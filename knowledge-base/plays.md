# GTM Plays

The source for the app's **Plays** section: practical best-practice guides distilled from the research.
Each play has a "when to use it", a few concrete steps (often with a detail under each), and a source.
This is the how-to layer, the counterpart to the glossary (the language) and benchmarks (the numbers).

Format (parsed by `app/scripts/build-data.mjs`): `## Area`, `### Play`, then `- when:`, repeated
`- step:` (each optionally followed by `- detail:`), and `- source:` lines.

## Foundations
### Define your beachhead (ECP before ICP)
- when: Before any outbound or spend, when you need to choose who to sell to first.
- step: Start from the customer you can win now, not the one you eventually want.
- detail: The broad Ideal Customer Profile is who you want at scale. The Early Customer Profile is the narrower group you can actually close in the next 3 to 18 months: short sales cycle, a pain they feel today, budget, and a reason to move now. Sell to the ECP first; the ICP comes later.
- step: Look for where you have an unfair advantage.
- detail: The easiest beachhead is one where you already have an edge: a founder's background, a warm network, a niche you understand, or a use case where your product is obviously the best fit. Win where you are strong before you fight uphill.
- step: Narrow it to three concrete attributes.
- detail: Describe the segment so specifically you could build a list from it, for example "Series A marketplaces, US, with an in-house sales team". If you cannot turn it into a list filter, it is still too broad.
- step: Prefer a segment with a visible trigger.
- detail: The best beachheads share an observable event or situation (just raised, just hired a role, hit a compliance deadline) that tells you when to reach out and what to say. A segment you can time beats one you can only describe.
- step: Sanity-check the size and reachability.
- detail: The segment needs enough accounts to build on, and they need to be findable and contactable. A perfect profile of 40 companies you cannot reach is not a beachhead.
- step: Commit, and say no to the rest for now.
- detail: Win this one segment completely before expanding. Spreading early splits your message, your data, and your learning. You expand from a beachhead you own, not from a dozen you dabble in.
- source: Maja Voje (Early Customer Profile); GTM Strategist 2025 (n=195)

### Pick your motion by ACV
- when: Deciding how to go to market, before you hire reps or buy ad spend.
- step: Under ~$5K ACV: lead with product-led self-serve plus warm, founder-led outbound.
- step: $5K to $25K ACV: hybrid, self-serve entry with sales-assist on expansion.
- step: Over ~$25K ACV: account-based (ABX) and sales-led, with multi-threaded outbound.
- step: Under $1M ARR, your best channels are LinkedIn, warm outbound, and founder brand. Hold paid ads, conferences, and heavy ABX until later.
- source: GTM Strategist 2025 (Voje + Poyar, n=195)

### Nail your positioning
- when: After you have a beachhead, before you write any message, page, or pitch. Positioning is the input to all of them.
- step: List the alternatives your buyer is actually weighing, including doing nothing.
- detail: Buyers do not compare you to your ideal vision. They compare you to their current workaround (a spreadsheet, a competitor, a manual process) and to inaction. Your positioning only means anything relative to those real alternatives, so name them honestly first.
- step: Pick the few attributes where you genuinely win.
- detail: Name the two or three things you do that the alternatives do not, that a buyer in your beachhead actually cares about. This is not a feature list; it is the short set of unique, valued differences.
- step: Translate each attribute into the value a buyer feels.
- detail: For every differentiator, ask "so what?" until you reach a business outcome: time saved, revenue won, risk removed. Buyers buy the outcome, not the feature that produces it.
- step: Lead with risk removed, not upside.
- detail: Roughly 80% of buyers act to avoid pain or reduce risk, not to chase upside. Frame your value as removing a specific risk or the cost of the status quo. The "art of the possible" pitch mostly lands with other founders, not real buyers.
- step: Compress it into one sentence and one frame.
- detail: Write "We are the [familiar reference] of [your category]" and "For [beachhead], we [outcome] by [mechanism]". If you cannot say it in a sentence, your buyer cannot repeat it to their boss.
- step: Pressure-test it on a real buyer.
- detail: Say it out loud to someone in the beachhead. If they get defensive, confused, or say "so what", it is not done. Positioning is validated by their reaction, not by your conviction.
- source: April Dunford (Obviously Awesome); Jean Grosser (sell risk reduction)

### Design your offer
- when: Once positioning is clear, before outreach. The offer is the concrete thing you put in front of a buyer.
- step: Write the offer as one sentence: who, outcome, mechanism.
- detail: "For [beachhead], we [deliver this specific outcome] by [the mechanism that makes it work]". Specific beats broad. "We help teams with sales" is not an offer; "we hand franchise vendors a verified list of operator contacts in a day" is.
- step: Know whether you are capturing demand or generating it.
- detail: Demand capture serves a need the buyer already feels and is shopping for, and it is easier to sell. Demand generation paints an outcome they did not know to want, which is harder and needs more education. Lead with whichever your offer actually is.
- step: Make the value concrete, and quantify it where you can.
- detail: Replace adjectives with specifics: time saved, dollars won, risk removed, a number. Vague value ("save time, grow faster") gives no reason to act now.
- step: Find your unique mechanism, the "why you" inside the offer.
- detail: Name the specific thing about how you deliver that makes you hard to compare. That is what keeps you out of a race to the bottom on price and lets you be a category of one.
- step: Reduce the friction of the first step.
- detail: The call to action should be a small, concrete next action (a sample, a one-pager, five free brands), not "book a 30-minute demo to learn about value". Match the ask to what a cold buyer will actually do.
- step: Test offers; do not marry the first one.
- detail: You uncover the offer the market wants by testing several framings against real buyers, not by perfecting one on a whiteboard. Stay agnostic about which should win and let replies decide.
- source: Jordan Crawford (stealth offer, category of one); demand capture vs generation (practitioner)

## Channels and lists
### Choose your channel
- when: After positioning and offer, when you decide where to reach your beachhead. Pick one to start.
- step: Match the channel to your ACV and motion.
- detail: Low ACV (under ~$5K) cannot fund high-touch sales, so lead with self-serve, warm or signal-based outbound, and SEO/content. High ACV (over ~$25K) justifies account-based and sales-led. Do not run an expensive channel on a cheap product.
- step: Sort channels by role, not just by name.
- detail: Discovery (how they first hear: outbound, content, community), capture (high-intent, in-market: branded and comparison search), conversion (free to paid), and expansion (upsell). A plan that is all discovery and no capture or conversion leaks.
- step: Prefer compounding channels for scale, linear ones for learning.
- detail: Compounding channels (SEO/AEO, a content engine, an integration that puts you inside a buyer's workflow) keep paying off. Linear channels (each cold email reaches one person once) are for learning and early signal, not scale.
- step: Start with exactly one channel.
- detail: Pick the single highest-fit channel and run it properly before adding another. Running everything at once gives you no clean read on what actually works.
- step: Hold paid until the funnel converts organically.
- detail: Paid ads scale a funnel; they do not fix one. For a low-ACV product, paying for an unproven funnel burns money (CAC above LTV). Earn organic or warm conversions first, then use paid to scale.
- source: GTM Strategist 2025 (motion-fit); allbound channel framing (FullFunnel)

### Build your first list
- when: Once you have a beachhead and a channel, before you write outreach. Outbound is only as good as the list.
- step: Write the list spec before you pull anyone.
- detail: State the exact filters: the account firmographics, the title of the buyer, and the signal or situation that qualifies them. A clear spec is what prevents a generic, low-fit list.
- step: Target the person who actually buys, not just the brand.
- detail: Get to the real decision-maker or champion, not a generic inbox or the company HQ. The right contact at the right account beats more contacts at the wrong level.
- step: Build the first list by hand, even if it is slow.
- detail: Building ~100 rows manually teaches you what good fit and good data look like, and surfaces the signals worth automating later. Automate only after you know what you are looking for.
- step: Enrich and find contacts with a waterfall.
- detail: Use a data tool (Clay, Apollo) or a sequence of email finders to get verified contacts. Trying multiple providers in order raises your hit rate versus relying on any single one.
- step: Verify every address before it touches your sender.
- detail: Run the list through a verifier (MillionVerifier, NeverBounce) to drop invalid and risky addresses. Bouncing on a fresh domain is the fastest way to wreck deliverability.
- step: Keep the list honest.
- detail: No fabricated or guessed contacts. Dedupe, and respect the public-record and platform boundaries on where the data came from. Bad data is a legal and reputation risk, not just a bounce.
- source: Clay and Apollo (enrichment); practitioner list-building best practice

## Outbound
### Run signal-based outbound
- when: You want replies instead of spray-and-pray, and you can see buying signals.
- step: Choose signals that actually predict a need.
- detail: Good signals correlate with buying: funding, a new exec in the relevant role, hiring for a related job, a product launch, a public filing, a bad review, a tech change. Avoid weak signals (a generic "is hiring") that tell you nothing specific about their situation.
- step: Abstract the situation behind the signal, not just the event.
- detail: The signal is the trigger; the situation is the story. "Hired a Python dev and has no DevOps" is a situation you can speak to. "Is hiring" is not. Reach for the underlying state your product addresses.
- step: Reach out while the signal is fresh.
- detail: Relevance decays fast. A signal acted on this week lands; the same signal in two months is stale. Build a way to catch signals continuously and move quickly, because timing is most of the advantage.
- step: Tailor the message to the specific situation.
- detail: The opening should make it obvious you reached out because of their situation, not a template merge. Reference the specific thing, then connect it to a concrete outcome you can deliver for that situation.
- step: Split the list by the data point that changes the pitch.
- detail: One signal can map to several situations. Segment by the variable that changes your message (team size, tech stack, whether they have a function in-house) so each group gets a relevant version, not a lowest-common-denominator one.
- step: Do not treat the signal as the whole reason.
- detail: The signal gets you in the door at the right time; it is not the entire pitch. Pair it with a real value proposition, or you are just saying "I saw you are hiring, buy my thing".
- source: Maja Voje (signals); Jordan Crawford (situation-based targeting)

### Write a permissionless value message (PVP)
- when: Crafting the actual cold message, especially for a higher-value account.
- step: Start from a Pain-Qualified Segment, not firmographics.
- detail: Build the list around an observable pain in public data (a quality penalty, a missing role, a bad-review pattern), not just "companies of size X in industry Y". You are targeting people who demonstrably have the problem, not who merely fit a profile.
- step: Do the work upfront and hand over real value.
- detail: A permissionless value prop gives something useful before asking for anything: a decision-maker card, a short list of accounts they should target, a specific finding about their market. The bar is "would they pay to receive this?" If not, it is not yet a PVP.
- step: Open with the specific, true thing you found.
- detail: Lead with the concrete observation about them or their market, with the source and date. Specificity is what proves you are not blasting a template. Vague flattery or "I hope this finds you well" gets deleted.
- step: Keep it short, plain, and human.
- detail: A few sentences. No jargon, no "synergies", no walls of text. Write the way a smart person emails a busy person they respect, and cut anything that reads as AI or as marketing.
- step: Make the call to action a small, concrete next step.
- detail: Ask for one specific, low-effort action ("want the full list?", "I can send the one-pager") rather than "hop on a 30-minute call about value". Match the ask to what a cold, skeptical buyer will actually do.
- step: Verify every claim and contact, and gut-check the honesty.
- detail: Every number, name, and quote must be real and current; a single wrong "verified" fact burns the trust the whole message runs on. Read it back as the recipient: does any line make you defensive or sound made up? Cut it.
- source: Jordan Crawford (Cannonball / Blueprint GTM); honesty gate

## Deliverability
### Set up cold-email deliverability
- when: Before you send any cold volume. For cold outbound, this infrastructure usually matters more than the copy.
- step: Never send cold email from your primary domain.
- detail: Cold outbound generates spam complaints and bounces. If those hit yourcompany.com, your real business email (sales replies, invoices, the whole team's day-to-day) starts landing in spam, and domain reputation is slow and painful to rebuild. The fix is to isolate cold sending onto separate domains you can afford to burn, so a reputation hit is contained.
- step: Buy a few "lookalike" sending domains and redirect them to your main site.
- detail: If your primary is acme.com, register close variants like getacme.com, tryacme.com, or acme.io. Prefer .com from a reputable registrar, and set a 301 redirect from each to acme.com so they look legitimate to recipients and filters. Use a separate root domain, not a subdomain of your primary, so the main domain stays fully insulated.
- step: Create only a few mailboxes per domain, and cap volume per mailbox.
- detail: About 3 mailboxes per domain is a safe rule of thumb. After warmup, keep each mailbox to roughly 20 to 50 cold emails per day. So to send ~300 per day you need around 8 to 10 mailboxes across ~3 domains, not one inbox blasting 300. The sending platform rotates across all of them.
- step: Authenticate every sending domain.
- detail: Google and Yahoo require SPF, DKIM, and a DMARC policy (p=none is the floor), with the From header aligned to SPF or DKIM. Set these DNS records on each domain before you send, plus MX records and your own tracking subdomain rather than the tool's shared one. This is settled, enforced policy, not a nice-to-have.
- step: Warm up each mailbox for 2 to 4 weeks before sending cold.
- detail: A warmup tool automatically exchanges and replies to seed emails to build a positive sending history, then you ramp real volume gradually. Sending cold from a brand-new mailbox is the fastest way to get flagged. Most sending platforms (Instantly, Smartlead) include warmup.
- step: Verify the list, watch your complaint rate, and monitor reputation.
- detail: Run every address through a verifier (MillionVerifier, NeverBounce, ZeroBounce) to drop invalid and risky ones; keep bounces under roughly 2 to 3%. Keep spam complaints under 0.3% (target under 0.1%), include a working one-click unsubscribe honored within two days, and watch Google Postmaster Tools so you can rotate out any mailbox whose reputation drops.
- step: Expect Microsoft (Outlook) to be harder than Gmail.
- detail: Outlook and Office 365 filter more aggressively and give far less feedback. For Microsoft-heavy lists, warm up longer, send slower, or split campaigns by recipient provider so a weak Outlook result does not drag down the rest.
- source: Google and Yahoo sender guidelines (settled, 2024+); the domain, mailbox, and warmup tactics are practitioner best practice, not from a single primary source

### Stay compliant (CAN-SPAM, CASL, GDPR)
- when: Any outbound email, especially if you expand beyond the US.
- step: US (CAN-SPAM): truthful headers and subject, a valid physical address, and a working opt-out you honor promptly. B2B cold email is legal if you meet these.
- step: Canada (CASL): much stricter, generally consent-based. Treat Canadian sending as needing a real basis.
- step: EU and UK (GDPR/PECR): you need a lawful basis such as legitimate interest, data minimization, and easy opt-out. The "it is just B2B" reasoning does not cover EU/UK people.
- step: When in doubt on EU/UK/Canada, get real counsel rather than guessing.
- source: Google/Yahoo + CAN-SPAM/CASL/GDPR practitioner guidance (flagged for verification)

## Inbound
### Programmatic SEO and answer-engine optimization
- when: You have a dataset or many entities and want compounding inbound for a low-ACV product.
- step: Generate a templated page per entity (for example one per brand), but give each page genuine, unique value so it is not thin content.
- step: Build comparison and "alternative" pages for high-intent, in-market searchers.
- step: Optimize to be the cited source when someone asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google AI Overviews, not just to rank in classic search.
- step: For a data product, the data itself is the content and the moat.
- source: Practitioner guidance (AEO is still evolving; verify tactics before scaling)

## Testing
### Run a fixed-window outbound test
- when: Before you scale or automate any new channel or message.
- step: Test one channel at a time. Never two at once.
- detail: If you run cold email, LinkedIn, and ads together, you cannot tell which one worked. Isolate a single channel per test so the result is attributable. This is the most-broken rule in early GTM.
- step: Hold the offer and segment fixed; vary one thing.
- detail: A test only teaches you something if one variable changes. Keep the beachhead and offer constant and test the message, or keep the message constant and test the segment. Changing everything at once gives you noise.
- step: Run enough volume to mean something.
- detail: At 100 sends you might book one meeting by luck; at ~1,000 activities on one channel you start to see something real. Do not kill or scale a test on a handful of sends. Define the window and volume before you start.
- step: Judge on outcomes, not vanity metrics.
- detail: Opens are unreliable and sends are an input, not a result. Judge on replies and especially meetings booked. A useful bar for a pain-based test is roughly a 1% meeting-booked rate; below that, the problem is usually the list or the offer, not the copy.
- step: Validate by hand before you automate.
- detail: Send the first batch manually and read every reply, including the negative ones. The replies tell you what is broken (wrong list, wrong pain, weak offer) faster than any dashboard. Automate only the step that has proven it works.
- step: Decide on the evidence: scale, fix, or kill.
- detail: At the end of the window, make a call. Beat the bar: scale or automate that one step. Close but not there: fix the single weakest link (usually list or offer) and rerun. Far off: kill it and try a different angle. Do not let a losing test limp on.
- source: Cannonball GTM "Outbound Campaign Testing Bible" (Jordan Crawford); validate-before-scale

## Measurement
### Measure what matters
- when: As soon as you start running anything. You cannot improve what you do not measure.
- step: Build a metric tree, top to bottom.
- detail: Start from the outcome (revenue or pipeline) and break it into the chain that produces it: contacts to replies to meetings to opportunities to closed deals. Each stage has a conversion rate you can watch and improve in isolation.
- step: Separate leading from lagging indicators.
- detail: Lagging metrics (revenue, closed deals) tell you what already happened. Leading metrics (replies, meetings booked) move first and let you act now. Pick a leading indicator to watch day to day.
- step: Judge on outcomes, not vanity metrics.
- detail: Open rate is unreliable (Apple Mail Privacy inflates it) and impressions do not pay. For outbound, the metric that matters is meetings booked, not opens or emails sent.
- step: Compare to a real benchmark, with its caveat.
- detail: Know what good looks like. For low-ACV cold outbound a ~3% reply rate is realistic, and a ~1% meeting-booked rate is a useful bar for a test. Treat benchmarks as context, not guarantees, and check the sample behind any number.
- step: Instrument once, then watch the trend.
- detail: Choose the one or two leading indicators that predict pipeline for your motion, track them consistently in one place, and watch the trend over time rather than reacting to single data points.
- source: Hunter 2026 (reply benchmarks); Cannonball (meeting-booked bar); RevOps practice

## GTM engineering
### Build a GTM system with skills
- when: You want to automate GTM work with AI without it becoming a fragile mess.
- step: Use the modern stack: AI for reasoning (Claude), a real data source, and a thin orchestration layer (Clay, n8n, or code). AI is commoditized; your data and context are the edge.
- step: Codify each repeatable task as a skill (a small standard operating procedure), not a one-off prompt.
- step: Decompose work to the smallest reusable unit, then build one agent or skill per unit.
- step: Deploy, do not destroy: keep automation to net-new, read-only work and keep a human gate before anything sends or writes to a CRM.
- source: Clay (four-layer stack); Jordan Crawford (deploy not destroy); Nick Saraev (skills as SOPs)
